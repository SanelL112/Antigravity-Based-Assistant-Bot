## 1.12 Assumptions and Conventions on the SAT

The SAT operates under a set of standard assumptions that you should be aware of. Unless the problem explicitly states otherwise, you should assume the following:

### Figure and Diagram Assumptions

1. **Figures are drawn to scale** unless explicitly noted otherwise. This is a significant advantage--you can use diagrams to estimate answers, check your work, or even measure angles and lengths on screen (though this is less reliable on digital).

2. **If a figure is noted as "not drawn to scale,"** do not trust its appearance. Draw your own sketch on scratch paper if needed.

3. **Geometric figures lie in a plane** unless the problem specifies otherwise (e.g., 3D geometry problems).

4. **The word "line" means a straight line**, and the word "line segment" means a finite portion of a line between two endpoints.

### Mathematical Assumptions

5. **"Average" means arithmetic mean** unless otherwise specified.

6. **"Percent" means out of 100.**

7. **Variables represent real numbers** unless otherwise specified.

8. **The domain of a function is the set of all real numbers for which the function is defined**, unless the problem specifies a restricted domain.

9. **"Distinct" means different.** If the problem says "two distinct real roots," it means two different real roots (discriminant > 0, not ≥ 0).

### Notation Conventions

10. **$i = \sqrt{-1}$** and $i^2 = -1$ for complex number problems.

11. **$\overline{AB}$** denotes the line segment from point $A$ to point $B$, and $AB$ denotes its length.

12. **$\angle A$** denotes the measure of angle $A$ (in degrees, unless radians are specified).

13. **$\Delta ABC$** denotes triangle $ABC$.

## 1.13 Building Your Study Plan: A Framework

Now that you understand the structure and strategies of the SAT, you need a plan to prepare effectively. Below is a framework for building a study plan that maximizes your score improvement.

### Phase 1: Diagnostic (Week 1)

1. **Take a full-length, timed practice SAT** under realistic conditions. Use an official College Board practice test available through Bluebook.
2. **Score the test** and analyze your results by content domain and question type.

5. **Gradually introduce timing.** Once you are accurate, start practicing under timed conditions.

1. **Review your error log.** Go through every error you have made during your preparation and make sure you understand the correct approach.

3. **Take 1–2 final practice tests** to build confidence and maintain your test-taking stamina.
4. **Focus on rest and mental preparation.** Do not cram the night before. Get a full night's sleep, eat well, and approach the test with confidence.

## 1.14 The Role of Practice: Quality Over Quantity

A critical distinction in SAT preparation is between **mindless practice** and **deliberate practice.** Mindless practice--solving hundreds of problems without analyzing errors or adjusting your approach--yields diminishing returns. Deliberate practice--focusing on your weaknesses, analyzing every error, and continuously refining your strategy--yields exponential improvement.

**Principles of deliberate practice:**

1. **Set specific goals for each study session.** Not "I will study SAT math," but "I will master solving systems of equations using elimination and substitution, and I will complete 10 problems with at least 90% accuracy."

2. **Focus on your weaknesses.** It is comfortable to practice what you are already good at, but it is not productive. The fastest score improvements come from addressing your weakest areas.

3. **Analyze every error.** For every problem you miss, determine: Was it a careless error? A conceptual error? A strategic error? What specific action will you take to prevent this error in the future?

4. **Vary your practice.** Do not always practice the same type of problem. Mix topics and difficulty levels to build flexibility and adaptability.

5. **Simulate test conditions regularly.** Practice under timed conditions, in a quiet environment, with the same tools you will use on test day (Desmos calculator, scratch paper).

6. **Track your progress.** Keep a log of your practice test scores, error patterns, and study hours. This data will help you identify what is working and what needs to change.

## 1.15 Understanding the Adaptive Test: A Deeper Dive

Because the adaptive nature of the Digital SAT is so central to your strategy, it deserves a deeper examination.

### How the Adaptation Works

The College Board has not disclosed the exact algorithm, but the general mechanism is well understood:

- **Module 1** contains a pre-assembled set of questions with a range of difficulties. Your performance on this module is evaluated based on both the number of correct answers and the difficulty of the questions you answered correctly.
- Based on this evaluation, the system selects **Module 2** from a pool of pre-assembled modules at different difficulty levels.
- Your final score is calculated based on your performance across both modules, with the difficulty level of the questions factored in.

### Strategic Implications

1. **Every question in Module 1 matters.** There is no way to know which questions are "more important" for the adaptation, so treat every question with equal care.

2. **Do not try to "game" the system by intentionally performing poorly on Module 1.** The easier Module 2 has a significantly lower score ceiling. A student who gets 20/22 correct on an easy Module 2 will score lower than a student who gets 18/22 correct on a hard Module 2.

3. **If you are unsure about a question in Module 1, make your best effort and move on.** Do not spend excessive time agonizing over a single question at the expense of others.

4. **The adaptive nature means that your experience on test day will be unique to you.** Do not compare your test to someone else's. The questions you see are tailored to your ability level.

## 1.16 The Desmos Advantage: A Strategic Deep Dive

The built-in Desmos calculator is arguably the most powerful tool available to you on the Digital SAT. Many students underutilize it, either because they are not familiar with its capabilities or because they default to algebraic solutions out of habit. Developing Desmos proficiency can save you significant time and reduce errors.

### Desmos Techniques for SAT Math

**Technique 1: Solving Systems of Equations**

For any system of equations, you can graph both equations in Desmos and find the intersection point(s). This works for:
- Two linear equations
- A linear and a quadratic equation
- Two quadratic equations
- Any combination of equations that can be expressed as $y = f(x)$

*Example:* To solve $y = 3x - 5$ and $y = x^2 - 2x + 1$, simply type both equations into Desmos. The intersection point(s) give you the solution(s).

**Technique 2: Finding Roots/Zeros of Functions**

To find where a function equals zero, graph the function and identify the $x$-intercepts. You can also type $y = 0$ as a second equation and find the intersection.

*Example:* To find the roots of $f(x) = x^2 - 5x + 6$, graph the function and note where it crosses the $x$-axis.

**Technique 3: Evaluating Functions at Specific Values**

Type the function definition (e.g., $f(x) = 2x^2 - 3x + 7$) and then type $f(4)$ to evaluate it at $x = 4$. You can also create a table of values.

**Technique 4: Finding the Vertex of a Parabola**

Graph a quadratic function and use Desmos to identify the maximum or minimum point. You can also use the "maximum" or "minimum" feature in the Desmos menu.

**Technique 5: Solving Equations Graphically**

To solve an equation like $2x + 5 = x^2 - 3$, graph $y = 2x + 5$ and $y = x^2 - 3$ separately and find the intersection. The $x$-coordinate of the intersection is the solution.

**Technique 6: Statistical Calculations**

Enter data into a Desmos table and use built-in functions to calculate mean, median, and standard deviation.

**Technique 7: Checking Your Work**

After solving a problem algebraically, use Desmos to verify your answer. If your algebraic solution says $x = 5$, graph the relevant function and check that it satisfies the conditions at $x = 5$.

### Building Desmos Fluency

Spend 15–20 minutes per day for two weeks practicing Desmos. By the end of this period, you should be able to:
- Graph any function in under 10 seconds
- Find intersections in under 5 seconds
- Create tables of values in under 15 seconds
- Use Desmos to verify algebraic solutions

This investment of time will pay significant dividends on test day.

## 2.1 Introduction: Why Linear Functions Dominate the SAT

Linear functions are the single most tested algebraic concept on the SAT. They appear in nearly every module, in every content domain, and in a staggering variety of disguises. A question about a line might be presented as a bare equation, a graph, a table of values, a word problem about a plumber's fee, a system of two equations, or even a geometric figure on the coordinate plane. Mastery of linear functions is not merely one topic among many -- it is the foundational skill upon which a large fraction of your math score depends.

This chapter will take you from the most basic definition of slope through the most nuanced contextual interpretations the SAT can devise. Every form of a line, every method of finding slope, every trick for interpreting intercepts, and every common trap will be explored in exhaustive detail.

---

## 2.2 The Concept of a Function

Before discussing linear functions specifically, we must understand what a function is in the first place.

### 2.2.1 Definition of A Function

A **function** is a relationship between two variables -- typically called the **input** (or independent variable, usually $x$) and the **output** (or dependent variable, usually $y$) -- such that **each input maps to exactly one output**.

This "exactly one output" condition is called the **vertical line test**: if you can draw any vertical line that intersects a graph at more than one point, that graph does not represent a function.

### 2.2.2 Function Notation

The SAT uses function notation extensively. When we write:

$$f(x) = 3x + 7$$

we are saying: "There is a function named $f$. Its input is $x$. To produce the output, multiply the input by 3 and add 7."

- $f(2) = 3(2) + 7 = 13$
- $f(a) = 3a + 7$
- $f(x + 1) = 3(x + 1) + 7 = 3x + 10$

**Critical SAT nuance:** $f(k) = 0$ means "the input value $k$ produces an output of 0." In graphical terms, $k$ is the $x$-intercept. This connection between function notation and intercepts is tested frequently.

### 2.2.3 Domain and Range

- **Domain:** The set of all possible input values ($x$-values).
- **Range:** The set of all possible output values ($y$-values).

For a linear function with no restrictions, the domain and range are both all real numbers. However, in word problems, the domain is often restricted by context (e.g., time cannot be negative, the number of items must be a whole number).

---

## 2.3 What Makes a Function "Linear"?

A **linear function** is any function that can be written in the form:

$$y = mx + b$$

or equivalently:

$$f(x) = mx + b$$

where $m$ and $b$ are constants. The graph of any linear function is a **straight line**.

### 2.3.1 Characteristics of Linear Functions

1. **Constant rate of change:** The output changes by the same amount for every 1-unit increase in the input. This constant rate of change is the slope.
2. **Straight-line graph:** The graph is always a straight line -- never curved.
3. **Degree 1:** The highest power of $x$ is 1. There are no $x^2$, $x^3$, $\sqrt{x}$, or $\frac{1}{x}$ terms.
4. **One $y$-intercept:** A linear function crosses the $y$-axis exactly once (except for vertical lines, which are not functions).

### 2.3.2 How the SAT Tests Whether a Relationship Is Linear

The SAT may present data in a table and ask whether the relationship is linear. To determine this:

- Check whether the **change in $y$** is constant for each **constant change in $x$**.
- If $x$ increases by the same amount each time and $y$ also changes by the same amount each time, the relationship is linear.

**Example of a linear table:**

| $x$ | $y$ |
|-----|-----|
| 0 | 5 |
| 2 | 9 |
| 4 | 13 |
| 6 | 17 |

Here, $x$ increases by 2 each time, and $y$ increases by 4 each time. The rate of change is constant: $\frac{4}{2} = 2$. This is linear.

**Example of a non-linear table:**

| $x$ | $y$ |
|-----|-----|
| 0 | 3 |
| 1 | 5 |
| 2 | 9 |
| 3 | 15 |

Here, $y$ changes by 2, then 4. The rate of change is not constant. This is not linear (it is quadratic).

---

## 2.4 Slope: The Defining Feature of a Line

### 2.4.1 Definition of Slope

**Slope** is a number that describes the steepness and direction of a line. It is the ratio of the **vertical change** (rise) to the **horizontal change** (run) between any two points on the line.

$$m = \frac{\text{rise}}{\text{run}} = \frac{\text{change in } y}{\text{change in } x}$$

### 2.4.2 The Slope Formula

Given two points $(x_1, y_1)$ and $(x_2, y_2)$ on a line:

$$m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}$$

**Critical details about using this formula:**

1. **Consistency of labeling is paramount.** You must subtract the coordinates in the same order in the numerator and denominator. If you start with $y_2$ in the numerator, you must start with $x_2$ in the denominator. Both of the following give the same answer:
   - $m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}$
   - $m = \frac{y_1 - y_2}{x_1 - x_2}$

   But mixing them -- for example, $m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_1 - x_2}$ -- gives the **wrong sign**.

2. **The slope is the same everywhere on the line.** Any two points on the same line will yield the same slope. This is a fundamental property of straight lines.

3. **Fraction simplification:** Always reduce the fraction. If the slope is $\frac{6}{4}$, write it as $\frac{3}{2}$.

### 2.4.3 Interpreting the Sign of the Slope

| Slope Sign | Direction of Line | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| $m > 0$ (positive) | Rises from left to right | As $x$ increases, $y$ increases |
| $m < 0$ (negative) | Falls from left to right | As $x$ increases, $y$ decreases |
| $m = 0$ | Horizontal | $y$ does not change as $x$ changes |
| $m$ undefined | Vertical | $x$ does not change; not a function |

### 2.4.4 Interpreting the Magnitude of the Slope

The **absolute value** of the slope tells you how steep the line is:

- A slope of $5$ is steeper than a slope of $2$.
- A slope of $-5$ is steeper than a slope of $2$ (steepness depends on absolute value, not sign).
- A slope of $\frac{1}{100}$ is nearly flat.
- A slope of $100$ is nearly vertical.

### 2.4.5 Slope as a Rate of Change

This is perhaps the most important concept for SAT word problems. The slope is not just a geometric property -- it is a **rate**.

If a line models a real-world situation, the slope tells you **how fast the dependent variable changes with respect to the independent variable**.

**Example:** If $y$ represents the total cost of a gym membership after $x$ months, and the equation is $y = 40x + 100$, then the slope $m = 40$ means the cost increases by $40 per month.

**Example:** If $y$ represents the remaining fuel in a car's tank after driving $x$ miles, and the equation is $y = -0.05x + 15$, then the slope $m = -0.05$ means the car uses 0.05 gallons of fuel per mile (or equivalently, gets 20 miles per gallon).

### 2.4.6 Finding Slope from a Graph

When given a graph:

1. Identify two points on the line that appear to fall at exact grid intersections.
2. Count the vertical change (rise) and horizontal change (run) between them.
3. Compute $m = \frac{\text{rise}}{\text{run}}$.
4. Determine the sign based on whether the line rises or falls from left to right.

**Pro tip:** Choose points that are far apart to minimize counting errors. Two points that are 1 unit apart horizontally are more prone to error than two points that are 5 or 10 units apart.

### 2.4.7 Finding Slope from an Equation

If the equation is in **slope-intercept form** ($y = mx + b$), the slope is simply the coefficient of $x$.

If the equation is in **standard form** ($Ax + By = C$), solve for $y$ to convert to slope-intercept form, or use the formula:

$$m = -\frac{A}{B}$$

**Derivation:** Starting from $Ax + By = C$, subtract $Ax$ from both sides: $By = -Ax + C$. Divide by $B$: $y = -\frac{A}{B}x + \frac{C}{B}$. Therefore, $m = -\frac{A}{B}$.

### 2.4.8 Finding Slope from a Table

Pick any two rows from the table and apply the slope formula. If the relationship is linear, every pair of points will give the same slope.

## 2.5 The Four Forms of a Linear Equation

The SAT expects you to work fluently with multiple forms of a linear equation. Each form has specific advantages, and the test frequently requires you to convert between them.

### 2.5.1 Slope-Intercept Form: $y = mx + b$

**Structure:**
- $m$ = slope
- $b$ = $y$-intercept (the $y$-value when $x = 0$)

**Best used for:**
- Graphing a line quickly
- Identifying the slope and $y$-intercept at a glance
- Comparing two lines (e.g., determining if they are parallel or perpendicular)
- Writing an equation when given the slope and $y$-intercept

**How to graph from slope-intercept form:**
1. Plot the $y$-intercept $(0, b)$ on the $y$-axis.
2. From that point, use the slope $m = \frac{\text{rise}}{\text{run}}$ to find a second point.
3. Draw a straight line through the two points.

**Example:** Graph $y = \frac{2}{3}x - 4$.
- $y$-intercept: $(0, -4)$. Plot this point.
- Slope: $\frac{2}{3}$. From $(0, -4)$, move up 2 units and right 3 units to reach $(3, -2)$. Plot this point.
- Draw the line through $(0, -4)$ and $(3, -2)$.

**Common SAT trap:** Students sometimes confuse $b$ with the $x$-intercept. Remember, $b$ is the $y$-intercept -- the value of $y$ when $x = 0$.

### 2.5.2 Point-Slope Form: $y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$

**Structure:**
- $m$ = slope
- $(x_1, y_1)$ = a specific point on the line

**Best used for:**
- Writing an equation when given the slope and one point
- Modeling situations where you know a starting value and a rate of change
- Word problems that give you a data point and a rate

**Why it works:** The slope between any point $(x, y)$ on the line and the known point $(x_1, y_1)$ must equal $m$. So:

$$\frac{y - y_1}{x - x_1} = m$$

Multiplying both sides by $(x - x_1)$ gives the point-slope form.

**Example:** Write the equation of the line with slope $-3$ that passes through $(4, -2)$.
