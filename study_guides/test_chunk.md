## 1.1 The Digital SAT: A Paradigm Shift

The SAT underwent a monumental transformation with the transition to the Digital SAT (DSAT), first administered internationally in 2023 and domestically in the United States beginning March 2024. This is not merely a change of medium--from paper to screen--but a fundamental reimagining of how the test is structured, administered, scored, and strategized. Understanding this new architecture is the single most important foundational step you can take before solving a single math problem. Every strategic decision you make during the test must be informed by the structure of the exam itself.

The Digital SAT is administered through the **Bluebook(TM) application**, College Board's proprietary testing platform, which runs on laptops, tablets, and school-managed Chromebooks. The application includes a built-in graphing calculator (Desmos), a countdown timer, the ability to flag questions for later review, and annotation tools. Familiarity with this interface before test day is not optional--it is a competitive advantage.

## 1.2 Section Architecture: The Two-Module System

### The Overall Math Section

The Math section of the Digital SAT is **70 minutes long** and is divided into **two modules**, each lasting **35 minutes**. Each module contains **22 questions**, for a total of **44 math questions**. This gives you an average of approximately **95 seconds per question**--but this average is deceptive, as we will discuss in the time management section below.

The two modules are not independent. They are connected by an adaptive mechanism that fundamentally changes how you should approach the test.

### Module 1: The Gatekeeper

Module 1 is a **mixed-difficulty module** containing a range of easy, medium, and hard questions. This module serves a singular, critical purpose: it determines the difficulty level of Module 2. The College Board describes this as a **"module-adaptive"** design (sometimes called "stage-adaptive"), which is distinct from the question-adaptive design of the old GRE.

**Why Module 1 matters disproportionately:**

- If you perform well on Module 1 (answering most questions correctly), you will receive a **harder Module 2**.
- If you perform poorly on Module 1, you will receive an **easier Module 2**.
- The harder Module 2 has a **higher score ceiling**--meaning the maximum score you can achieve is higher.
- The easier Module 2 has a **lower score ceiling**--meaning even if you answer every question correctly, your score will be capped at a lower value.

This creates a strategic imperative: **accuracy in Module 1 is the single most important factor in maximizing your score.** A student who rushes through Module 1 making careless errors, then cruises through an easy Module 2, will score lower than a student who is meticulous in Module 1 and then faces the challenge of a harder Module 2.

**Practical implication:** Do not rush Module 1. Do not sacrifice accuracy for speed in the first module. Every correct answer in Module 1 is an investment that pays dividends in the scoring potential of Module 2.

### Module 2: The Scorer

Module 2 adapts to your performance in Module 1. It contains **22 questions** at a difficulty level calibrated to your demonstrated ability. In the harder Module 2, questions tend to involve more complex reasoning, multi-step problems, and less straightforward applications of concepts. In the easier Module 2, questions are more direct but the scoring ceiling is lower.

**Key insight:** The adaptive nature of the test means that two students sitting next to each other may receive completely different Module 2 questions. Your test is, in a very real sense, customized to your ability level in real time.

## 1.3 Content Domains: What the SAT Tests

The College Board organizes the Math section into four content domains, each with an approximate weighting. Understanding these weightings helps you prioritize your study time.

### The Four Content Domains

| Domain | Approximate Weighting | Number of Questions (approx.) |
|---|---|---|
| **Algebra** | ~35% | ~15–16 questions |
| **Advanced Math** | ~35% | ~15–16 questions |
| **Problem Solving and Data Analysis** | ~15% | ~7 questions |
| **Geometry and Trigonometry** | ~15% | ~5–7 questions |

Let us examine each domain in detail.

#### Algebra (~35%)

This is the largest single domain and forms the backbone of the test. Topics include:

- **Linear equations and inequalities** (solving, graphing, interpreting)
- **Linear systems of equations** (solving, interpreting, determining number of solutions)
- **Linear functions** (notation, domain, range, transformations)
- **Linear modeling** (using linear functions to model real-world scenarios)
- **Absolute value** (basic equations and graphs)
- **Rearranging formulas** (isolating a variable)

The Algebra domain tests whether you can manipulate linear relationships fluently. This is not just about solving for $x$--it is about understanding what linear relationships mean, how they behave, and how to extract information from them.

#### Advanced Math (~35%)

This domain encompasses non-linear relationships and higher-order algebraic thinking:

- **Quadratic functions and equations** (factoring, completing the square, quadratic formula, vertex form, transformations)
- **Polynomial operations** (addition, subtraction, multiplication, division, factoring)
- **Rational expressions and equations** (simplifying, solving)
- **Radical expressions and equations** (simplifying, solving, extraneous solutions)
- **Exponential functions** (growth, decay, modeling, transformations)
- **Systems involving non-linear equations** (e.g., a linear and a quadratic equation)
- **Rational exponents and radicals**
- **Complex numbers** (basic arithmetic, $i^2 = -1$)
- **Polynomial Remainder Theorem**
- **Equivalent expressions** (rewriting expressions in different forms to reveal properties)

The Advanced Math domain is where most students encounter the hardest questions. It requires not just procedural fluency but conceptual depth--understanding *why* a quadratic has a certain number of roots, *why* an exponential function behaves differently from a linear one, and *how* to rewrite expressions strategically.

#### Problem Solving and Data Analysis (~15%)

This domain tests quantitative literacy--the ability to work with data, ratios, percentages, and probabilistic reasoning:

- **Ratios, rates, and proportions**
- **Percentages** (including percent change, percent increase/decrease, compound percent problems)
- **Units and unit conversion**
- **Scatterplots** (interpreting, lines of best fit, making predictions)
- **Two-way tables** (reading, interpreting, conditional relative frequencies)
- **Data interpretation** (bar graphs, line graphs, histograms, box plots)
- **Measures of center** (mean, median, mode) and their properties
- **Measures of spread** (range, interquartile range, standard deviation--conceptual understanding)
- **Probability** (basic probability, conditional probability)
- **Statistical sampling** (margin of error, random sampling, generalization)
- **Evaluating statistical claims** (observational studies vs. experiments, causation vs. correlation)

This domain is often underestimated by students who focus heavily on algebra and geometry. However, it represents a significant portion of the test and includes many questions that are straightforward if you know the concepts but very difficult if you do not.

#### Geometry and Trigonometry (~15%)

This domain covers geometric and trigonometric reasoning:

- **Lines and angles** (parallel lines, perpendicular lines, angle relationships)
- **Triangles** (properties, congruence, similarity, Pythagorean theorem, special right triangles, area, perimeter)
- **Polygons** (interior angle sum, regular polygons, area, perimeter)
- **Circles** (area, circumference, arc length, sector area, central angles, inscribed angles)
- **Coordinate geometry** (slope, distance, midpoint, equations of lines and circles)
- **Right triangle trigonometry** (sine, cosine, tangent, reciprocal functions, complementary angle identity)
- **Solid geometry** (volume and surface area of basic 3D shapes--typically only 1–2 questions)
- **Transformations** (reflections, rotations, translations)

Geometry and trigonometry questions tend to be concentrated in the latter portion of each module. They often require the application of multiple formulas or concepts in a single problem.

## 1.4 Question Types: Multiple Choice and Student-Produced Response

### Multiple Choice (75% of questions)

The vast majority of SAT math questions--approximately **33 out of 44**--are standard multiple-choice questions with **four answer choices** (A, B, C, D). Each question has exactly one correct answer.

**Strategic notes on multiple choice:**

- The four answer choices are not random. They are carefully designed to include **distractors**--answers that result from common mistakes. If you make a typical error, you will likely see your wrong answer among the choices. This is both a warning and a tool: knowing common mistakes helps you avoid them, and recognizing common distractors can help you identify traps.
- There is **no penalty for wrong answers.** You should **never leave a question blank.** Even a random guess has an expected value of 25% correct. If you can eliminate even one choice, your odds improve to 33%. If you can eliminate two, you are at 50%.
- Some multiple-choice questions have answer choices that are themselves expressions or equations, and you must determine which one is correct. These can sometimes be solved efficiently by **plugging in** the answer choices.

### Student-Produced Response / Grid-In (25% of questions)

Approximately **11 out of 44** questions are Student-Produced Response (SPR) questions, sometimes called "grid-ins." For these questions, you must calculate the answer yourself and enter it into a grid.

**Grid-in rules and conventions:**

- **Positive numbers only:** The grid can only accommodate positive numbers. If your answer is negative, you cannot grid it in (which means negative answers are never required for grid-in questions).
- **Maximum of 4 characters:** The grid can hold up to 4 characters. This means the maximum whole number you can enter is 9999.
- **Decimals:** You can enter decimals. For example, $0.666$ or $0.667$ for $2/3$. You should round or truncate at the **fourth decimal place.** For $2/3 = 0.666666...$, you would enter $0.666$ or $0.667$ (both are accepted).
- **Fractions:** You can enter fractions using the fraction slash. For example, $2/3$ can be entered as `2/3`. If a fraction requires more than 4 characters (e.g., $12/34$), you must convert it to a decimal.
- **Mixed numbers:** Mixed numbers like $3 \frac{1}{2}$ must be converted to improper fractions ($7/2$) or decimals ($3.5$). The grid does not accept mixed numbers.
- **No symbols:** Do not include dollar signs, percent signs, or variables in your answer.
- **Simplification:** Answers should be in simplest form.

**Strategic notes on grid-ins:**

- Since there are no answer choices to guide you, grid-ins require you to solve the problem completely on your own. There is no option to backsolve from choices.
- However, grid-ins also cannot include "trap" answer choices, so you are less likely to be led astray by a clever distractor.
- Grid-ins are an excellent opportunity to use the **Desmos calculator** to verify your answer before entering it.

## 1.5 The Calculator Policy: Desmos and Beyond

### The Built-In Desmos Calculator

One of the most significant features of the Digital SAT is the **built-in Desmos graphing calculator**, available for **every question** in the Math section. This is a powerful tool that can solve a remarkable number of problems quickly and accurately.

**What Desmos can do:**

- **Graph functions** (linear, quadratic, polynomial, exponential, trigonometric, etc.)
- **Find intersection points** of graphs (extremely useful for solving systems of equations)
- **Create tables of values** for functions
- **Evaluate expressions** at specific values
- **Perform statistical calculations** (mean, median, regression)
- **Solve equations** graphically by finding where a function equals a specific value

**What Desmos cannot do:**

- **Symbolic algebra** (it cannot factor expressions symbolically or expand polynomials algebraically)
- **CAS operations** (no computer algebra system functionality)
- **Symbolic differentiation or integration**

**Strategic implications:**

- For roughly **one-third of all math questions**, Desmos provides a viable solution path, and for some questions, it is the fastest method.
- You should practice with Desmos extensively before test day. The calculator is available within the Bluebook app and also at [desmos.com/calculator](https://www.desmos.com/calculator).
- Learn to use Desmos for: solving systems of equations (graph both and find intersection), finding roots of functions (graph and identify $x$-intercepts), evaluating functions (type expressions with specific $x$-values), and solving equations by graphing both sides.

### Approved Handheld Calculators

You may also bring an **approved handheld calculator** as a backup or supplement to Desmos. Approved calculators include most scientific calculators and graphing calculators (e.g., TI-84 Plus, TI-Nspire). However, calculators with internet connectivity, QWERTY keyboards, or stylus capability are not permitted.

**Recommendation:** Use Desmos as your primary calculator and a handheld scientific calculator as a backup. Desmos is faster for most operations once you are proficient with it.

## 1.6 Time Management: The 95-Second Myth

### The Average Time Budget

With 44 questions in 70 minutes, the average time per question is approximately **95 seconds** (1 minute and 35 seconds). However, this average is misleading because questions vary enormously in difficulty and time consumption.

### A Realistic Time Allocation

A more effective time management strategy allocates time based on question difficulty:

| Question Type | Suggested Time | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Easy questions | 30–60 seconds | These should be quick. Read carefully, solve, move on. |
| Medium questions | 60–95 seconds | Standard time allocation. |
| Hard questions | 95–150 seconds | These require more thought and may involve multiple steps. |
| Very hard / time-consuming | 150–180 seconds | Consider flagging and returning if you are stuck. |

### The Flag-and-Return Strategy

The Bluebook app allows you to **flag questions** for later review. This is an essential feature. If you encounter a question that you cannot solve within 2 minutes, **flag it and move on.** Do not sacrifice 5 easy questions at the end of the module because you spent 4 minutes on one hard question in the middle.

**Protocol for flagging:**

1. If you have spent more than 2 minutes on a question and are not certain of your answer, flag it.
2. If a question feels unusually difficult or confusing, flag it and move on.
3. After completing all questions in a module, return to flagged questions with your remaining time.
4. If time runs out, make sure all questions have an answer (never leave anything blank).

### The Two-Pass Method

Consider using a **two-pass approach** within each module:

**Pass 1:** Go through the module sequentially, answering every question you can solve confidently and quickly. Flag anything that feels difficult or time-consuming.

**Pass 2:** Return to flagged questions. With the pressure of having completed the rest of the module, you can focus your remaining time on the hardest questions.

This method ensures that you answer every "easy" question correctly before investing time in hard ones. It also prevents the catastrophic scenario of running out of time on easy questions at the end because you were stuck on a hard question in the middle.

### Module 1 vs. Module 2 Time Strategy

- **Module 1:** Be slightly more conservative with time. Accuracy here is paramount because it determines your Module 2 difficulty and score ceiling. It is worth spending an extra 15 seconds to double-check a Module 1 answer.
- **Module 2:** You can be slightly more aggressive with time, especially if you received the harder module (which means your score ceiling is already high). However, never be so rushed that you make careless errors.

## 1.7 The Scoring System: How Your Score Is Calculated

### Raw Score to Scaled Score

Your SAT Math score ranges from **200 to 800** and is part of your total score range of **400 to 1600**. The process of converting your raw score (number of questions correct) to a scaled score involves **equating**--a statistical process that accounts for the difficulty of the specific test form you took.

This means that the number of questions you can miss to achieve a given score varies slightly from test to test. A harder test form will be more forgiving (you can miss more questions and still get a high score) than an easier form.

### No Penalty for Wrong Answers

This cannot be overstated: **there is no penalty for incorrect answers.** Your raw score is simply the number of questions you answer correctly. An incorrect answer and a blank answer are scored identically (as zero points for that question).

**Therefore: Always answer every question. Never leave a blank.**

If you are running out of time and have unanswered questions, fill in a guess for every remaining question. Statistically, if you guess randomly on 5 questions, you will get approximately 1.25 correct on average. That is 1.25 more points than leaving them blank.

### The Adaptive Scoring Implications

Because of the adaptive module structure, the scoring algorithm takes into account not just how many questions you get right, but **which questions you get right.** Getting hard questions correct in Module 2 contributes more to your score than getting easy questions correct. This is why unlocking the harder Module 2 through strong Module 1 performance is so important.

**Rough scoring guidelines:**

- To score **700+**, you typically need to answer approximately **38–40+ questions correctly** out of 44, and you likely need the harder Module 2.
- To score **600+**, you typically need to answer approximately **30–34 questions correctly** out of 44.
- To score **500+**, you typically need to answer approximately **22–26 questions correctly** out of 44.

These are approximate and vary by test form. The key takeaway is that **every correct answer matters**, and the marginal value of each correct answer is higher at the upper end of the score distribution.

## 1.8 The Mindset: Psychological Preparation for a High Score

### The Confidence-Competence Loop

SAT math performance is not purely a function of mathematical knowledge. Psychological factors--confidence, anxiety management, focus, and strategic thinking--play a massive role in determining your score. Research in educational psychology consistently shows that test anxiety can significantly impair working memory, which is precisely the cognitive resource you need most during a timed math test.

**Building the loop:**

1. **Competence** (through thorough preparation) → builds → **Confidence** (belief in your ability)
2. **Confidence** → reduces → **Anxiety** (less fear of failure)
3. **Reduced anxiety** → improves → **Performance** (better focus, clearer thinking)
4. **Better performance** → reinforces → **Competence** (positive feedback loop)

### Managing Test Anxiety

**Before the test:**

- **Prepare thoroughly.** The single best antidote to anxiety is genuine competence. When you have solved hundreds of practice problems and understand every concept on the test, anxiety naturally diminishes.
- **Simulate test conditions.** Take full-length practice tests under realistic conditions (timed, quiet, no distractions). The more familiar the testing experience becomes, the less anxiety it provokes.
- **Develop a pre-test routine.** In the days before the test, maintain consistent sleep, nutrition, and exercise habits. Avoid cramming the night before--last-minute studying tends to increase anxiety more than it increases competence.
- **Visualize success.** Spend a few minutes each day mentally rehearsing yourself working through the test calmly and confidently. This technique, used by elite athletes and performers, has been shown to improve actual performance.

**During the test:**

- **Control your breathing.** If you feel anxiety rising, take 3–5 slow, deep breaths. This activates the parasympathetic nervous system and reduces the physiological symptoms of anxiety.
- **Use positive self-talk.** Replace thoughts like "I can't do this" with "I am prepared for this" or "I will do my best on this question and move on."
- **Focus on the current question.** Do not think about questions you have already answered or questions that are coming up. Give 100% of your attention to the question in front of you.
- **Accept imperfection.** You do not need a perfect score to achieve an excellent result. If a question is too hard, flag it, guess, and move on without guilt. One difficult question will not determine your score.

### The Growth Mindset

Students who approach SAT preparation with a **growth mindset** (the belief that mathematical ability can be developed through effort and practice) consistently outperform those with a **fixed mindset** (the belief that mathematical ability is innate and unchangeable). This is not merely motivational rhetoric--it is supported by decades of research by psychologist Carol Dweck and others.

**Practical implications:**

- When you encounter a problem you cannot solve, view it as an opportunity to learn, not as evidence of inadequacy.
- When you make an error on a practice problem, analyze the error systematically. What concept did you misunderstand? What step did you skip? How will you avoid this error in the future?
- Track your progress over time. As you see your practice scores improve, your belief in your ability to grow will be reinforced, creating a virtuous cycle.

## 1.9 Core Problem-Solving Strategies

The SAT is not just a test of mathematical knowledge--it is a test of **problem-solving ability.** Many questions can be solved through multiple approaches, and the ability to choose the most efficient approach is a skill that directly translates to a higher score. Below are the core strategies that you should internalize and practice until they become second nature.

### Strategy 1: Backsolving (Plugging in Answer Choices)

**When to use it:** When the question asks for a specific value and the answer choices are numbers (not expressions or variables).

**How it works:**

1. Start with answer choice **C** (the middle value).
2. Substitute this value into the problem and check if it satisfies the conditions.
3. If C is too large, eliminate C, B, and D (if the choices are ordered). If C is too small, eliminate C, A, and B.
4. Test the remaining choice or the next middle value.

**Example scenario:** "What value of $x$ satisfies $3x + 7 = 22$?" with answer choices A) 3, B) 4, C) 5, D) 6. Start with C: $3(5) + 7 = 22$. Correct! Answer is C.

**Advantages:** Converts an algebra problem into an arithmetic problem. Eliminates the risk of algebraic errors.

**Disadvantages:** Can be time-consuming if you need to test multiple choices. Less efficient when answer choices are expressions rather than numbers.

### Strategy 2: Plugging in Your Own Numbers

**When to use it:** When the problem involves variables in the question and answer choices, or when the problem describes a general relationship that you can make specific.

**How it works:**

1. Choose simple numbers for the variables in the problem (avoid 0, 1, and numbers already used in the problem).
2. Calculate the answer using your chosen numbers.
3. Substitute your numbers into each answer choice to see which one matches your calculated answer.

**Example scenario:** "If $x$ is doubled and then increased by 5, the result is 17. What is $x$?" Instead of solving algebraically, you could work backwards: if the answer is 6, then doubling 6 gives 12, and 12 + 5 = 17. Correct.

**Advantages:** Makes abstract problems concrete. Reduces the chance of algebraic errors.

**Disadvantages:** Can be time-consuming. If two answer choices match your first set of numbers, you need to choose different numbers and test again.

### Strategy 3: Estimation and Elimination

**When to use it:** When the answer choices are spread far apart, or when the problem asks for an approximate value.

**How it works:**

1. Round numbers in the problem to make mental calculations easier.
2. Calculate an approximate answer.
3. Eliminate answer choices that are far from your estimate.

**Example scenario:** "What is 37% of 483?" The answer choices are A) 158.71, B) 178.71, C) 198.71, D) 218.71. Estimate: 40% of 500 = 200. 37% of 483 should be slightly less than 200. The closest answer is B) 178.71.

**Advantages:** Extremely fast. Can eliminate 2–3 choices instantly on many problems.

**Disadvantages:** Not useful when answer choices are close together. Requires number sense and comfort with approximation.

### Strategy 4: Desmos Graphical Solutions

**When to use it:** When the problem involves equations, systems of equations, or functions that can be graphed.

**How it works:**

1. Enter the equations into Desmos.
2. Use the intersection feature to find solutions.
3. Use the table feature to evaluate functions at specific values.

**Example scenario:** "What is the solution to the system $y = 2x + 3$ and $y = x^2 - 4x + 7$?" Graph both equations in Desmos and find the intersection point(s).

**Advantages:** Fast, accurate, and eliminates algebraic errors. Works for a wide range of problem types.

**Disadvantages:** Requires familiarity with Desmos. Not useful for problems that cannot be expressed graphically.

### Strategy 5: Working Backwards from the Question

**When to use it:** When the problem describes a sequence of operations or transformations and asks for an initial value.

**How it works:**

1. Start with the final result.
2. Reverse each operation in reverse order.
3. Arrive at the initial value.

**Example scenario:** "A number is multiplied by 3, then 7 is subtracted, then the result is divided by 2, giving 11. What is the original number?" Work backwards: 11 × 2 = 22, 22 + 7 = 29, 29 ÷ 3 = 9.67.

**Advantages:** Intuitive and straightforward for sequential problems.

**Disadvantages:** Only applicable to certain problem types.

### Strategy 6: Identifying and Exploiting Symmetry

**When to use it:** When the problem involves symmetric functions, geometric figures with symmetry, or patterns that repeat.

**How it works:**

1. Recognize the symmetry in the problem.
2. Use the symmetry to reduce the amount of calculation needed.

**Example scenario:** "If $f(x) = x^2 - 4x + 3$, what is $f(5)$ and $f(-1)$?" Notice that $x = 2$ is the axis of symmetry (vertex). Both $x = 5$ and $x = -1$ are 3 units from the axis, so $f(5) = f(-1)$. Calculate one and you know both.

**Advantages:** Can dramatically speed up certain problems.

**Disadvantages:** Requires the ability to recognize symmetry, which is a developed skill.

### Strategy 7: Breaking Complex Problems into Simpler Parts

**When to use it:** When a problem seems overwhelming or involves multiple steps.

**How it works:**

1. Identify what the problem is ultimately asking.
2. Break the problem into smaller, manageable sub-problems.
3. Solve each sub-problem sequentially.
4. Combine the results to get the final answer.

**Example scenario:** "A rectangular garden has a length that is 3 more than twice its width. If the perimeter is 84 feet, what is the area of the garden?" Break it down: (1) Set up equations for length and width. (2) Use the perimeter formula to solve for width. (3) Calculate length. (4) Calculate area.

**Advantages:** Applicable to virtually every complex problem. Reduces errors by focusing on one step at a time.

**Disadvantages:** Requires discipline to avoid jumping ahead. Can be slower for simple problems that could be solved more directly.

## 1.10 Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

Understanding common errors is as important as understanding correct methods. The SAT is specifically designed to include answer choices that correspond to common mistakes. Below is a comprehensive taxonomy of errors and the strategies to prevent them.

### Category 1: Careless Errors

These are the most frustrating errors because you knew how to solve the problem but made a small mistake.

**Types of careless errors:**

- **Sign errors:** Forgetting a negative sign, especially when distributing (e.g., $-3(x - 2) = -3x + 6$, not $-3x - 6$).
- **Arithmetic errors:** Simple addition, subtraction, multiplication, or division mistakes.
- **Copy errors:** Writing down a number incorrectly from the problem or from one line of your work to the next.
- **Misreading the problem:** Answering for $x$ when the problem asks for $2x$, or finding the area when the problem asks for the perimeter.

**Prevention strategies:**

- **Write every step down.** Do not do arithmetic in your head. The extra 10 seconds you spend writing down a calculation can save you minutes of rework and the points lost to a careless error.
- **Circle what the problem is asking for.** Before you start solving, circle or underline the specific quantity you need to find. This prevents the "I solved for $x$ but the problem asked for $y$" error.
- **Check your answer by substitution.** If you solve for a value, plug it back into the original equation or problem to verify it works.
- **Use Desmos to verify.** When possible, use the calculator to check your algebraic answer.

### Category 2: Conceptual Errors

These errors result from a misunderstanding of a mathematical concept.

**Types of conceptual errors:**

- **Confusing percent increase with percent decrease:** A 20% increase followed by a 20% decrease does NOT return you to the original value. (If you start with 100, a 20% increase gives 120, and a 20% decrease of 120 gives 96.)
- **Confusing area scaling with length scaling:** If a side length doubles, the area quadruples (×4), not doubles (×2).
- **Confusing mean and median:** The mean is affected by outliers; the median is not.
- **Confusing correlation and causation:** Just because two variables are correlated does not mean one causes the other.
- **Forgetting the discriminant:** Assuming a quadratic always has two real roots without checking $b^2 - 4ac$.
- **Misapplying the Pythagorean theorem:** Using it on non-right triangles, or forgetting that $c$ must be the hypotenuse.

**Prevention strategies:**

- **Study concepts, not just procedures.** Do not merely memorize formulas--understand why they work and when they apply.
- **Create a "concept error log."** Every time you make a conceptual error in practice, write down the problem, your incorrect reasoning, and the correct reasoning. Review this log regularly.
- **Teach the concept to someone else.** If you cannot explain a concept clearly to another person, you do not fully understand it.

### Category 3: Strategic Errors

These errors result from poor test-taking strategy, not from a lack of mathematical knowledge.

**Types of strategic errors:**

- **Spending too long on one question:** Getting stuck on a hard question and sacrificing time for easier questions later.
- **Not using the calculator when it would help:** Trying to solve a system of equations by hand when Desmos could solve it in 10 seconds.
- **Not guessing when you should:** Leaving questions blank or spending too long on questions you are unlikely to solve.
- **Not reading all answer choices:** Selecting the first answer that seems correct without checking if a better answer exists.
- **Changing answers without good reason:** Research shows that students who change answers are more often changing from correct to incorrect than from incorrect to correct. Only change an answer if you have identified a specific error in your original reasoning.

**Prevention strategies:**

- **Practice time management.** During practice tests, use a timer and practice the flag-and-return strategy.
- **Develop a "2-minute rule."** If you have spent 2 minutes on a question and are not confident in your answer, flag it and move on.
- **Always review flagged questions.** Make sure you have time at the end of each module to revisit flagged questions.
- **Answer every question.** Fill in an answer for every question, even if you are guessing.

## 1.11 The Reference Sheet: What Is Provided and What Is Not

During the SAT Math section, you have access to a **reference sheet** that contains several formulas. Understanding what is on this sheet--and what is not--is critical for efficient preparation.

### What Is Provided on the Reference Sheet

The reference sheet includes:

- **Area of a circle:** $A = \pi r^2$
- **Circumference of a circle:** $C = 2\pi r$
- **Area of a triangle:** $A = \frac{1}{2}bh$
