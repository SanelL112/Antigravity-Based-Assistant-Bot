### ⏰ VERB TENSES: The Complete System (from 13_Tenses.pdf, 13_(Ans) Tenses.pdf)

The SAT tests **six primary verb tenses** plus perfect/progressive variations. Master the timeline and consistency rules.

#### The Six Core Tenses
| Tense | Form | Use | Signal Words |
|-------|------|-----|--------------|
| **Simple Present** | walk(s) | Habits, facts, general truths | always, usually, every day |
| **Simple Past** | walked | Completed past action | yesterday, ago, last year |
| **Simple Future** | will walk | Future action | tomorrow, will, shall |
| **Present Perfect** | has/have walked | Past action, present relevance | already, yet, since, for, ever, never |
| **Past Perfect** | had walked | Past-before-past | before, by the time, after |
| **Future Perfect** | will have walked | Future-before-future | by [future time] |

#### Progressive (Continuous) Forms
| Tense | Form | Use |
|-------|------|-----|
| **Present Progressive** | am/is/are walking | Happening now, temporary |
| **Past Progressive** | was/were walking | Ongoing in past |
| **Future Progressive** | will be walking | Ongoing at future time |
| **Present Perfect Progressive** | has/have been walking | Started past, continuing now |
| **Past Perfect Progressive** | had been walking | Ongoing before another past event |

#### SAT Tense Rules (College Panda Chapter 15)

**Rule 1: Tense Consistency**
- Don't shift tenses without a clear time marker
- Wrong: "She **walks** to school and **ate** breakfast."
- Right: "She **walks** to school and **eats** breakfast." OR "She **walked** to school and **ate** breakfast."

**Rule 2: Time Markers Dictate Tense**
| If you see... | Use... |
|---------------|--------|
| "by 2020" / "by the time" | Past Perfect (had + V3) |
| "since 2010" / "for 5 years" | Present Perfect (has/have + V3) |
| "last year" / "yesterday" / "ago" | Simple Past |
| "currently" / "now" / "at present" | Present Progressive |
| "will have [done] by [future]" | Future Perfect |

**Rule 3: Sequence of Tenses (Reported Speech)**
- Direct: "I **am** tired." → Reported: He said he **was** tired.
- Present → Past
- Past → Past Perfect
- Present Perfect → Past Perfect
- Will → Would

**Rule 4: Conditional Tense Sequences**
| Conditional Type | If-Clause | Main Clause |
|------------------|-----------|-------------|
| **Zero** (facts) | Present | Present |
| **First** (real future) | Present | Will + base |
| **Second** (unreal present) | Past | Would + base |
| **Third** (unreal past) | Past Perfect | Would + have + V3 |

#### Tense Selection Process (3-Step)
1. **Find the time marker** (or implied timeframe)
2. **Determine the relationship** to other verbs in the sentence
3. **Select the tense** that maintains logical sequence

#### Common SAT Tense Traps
| Trap | Example | Fix |
|------|---------|-----|
| **Unnecessary perfect** | "I have went to the store yesterday" | "I went to the store yesterday" |
| **Missing past perfect** | "By the time I arrived, the movie started" | "By the time I arrived, the movie **had started**" |
| **Tense shift in list** | "She likes reading, to write, and swimming" | Parallel: "reading, writing, and swimming" |
| **Future in time clause** | "When he will arrive, call me" | "When he **arrives**, call me" |

#### Practice (from 13_Tenses.pdf answers)
1. By the time the train **arrives**, we **will have waited** for an hour.
2. She **has been studying** since 3 PM. (Present Perfect Progressive)
3. If I **were** you, I **would accept** the offer. (Second conditional)
4. The report states that the economy **grew** by 3% last year. (Sequence: states → grew)

### 🎯 SUBJECT-VERB AGREEMENT: Advanced Traps (from 4_(Ans) Subject Verb Agreement.pdf)

#### Core Rule: Subject and verb must agree in NUMBER (singular/plural)

#### The SAT's Favorite Agreement Traps

**Trap 1: Intervening Prepositional Phrases**
- "The box **of chocolates is** open." (Subject = box, singular)
- "The changes **in the schedule are** confusing." (Subject = changes, plural)
- **Strategy**: Cross out prepositional phrases to find the true subject.

**Trap 2: Inverted Sentences (Verb before Subject)**
- "There **is** a book on the table." → "There **are** books on the table."
- "Here **comes** the bus." → "Here **come** the buses."
- "Never **have I seen** such beauty."
- **Strategy**: Find the subject AFTER the verb.

**Trap 3: Collective Nouns**
- **Singular** (usually): team, committee, family, group, jury, audience, class
- "The committee **has** decided." (acting as unit)
- **Plural** when members act individually: "The committee **are** arguing among themselves."

**Trap 4: Indefinite Pronouns**
| Always Singular | Always Plural | Context-Dependent |
|-----------------|---------------|-------------------|
| each, either, neither | both, few, many, several | all, any, most, none, some |
| one, everyone, everybody | | (depends on noun after "of") |
| someone, anybody, nobody | | |
| everything, something, nothing | | |

**Trap 5: Compound Subjects**
- **And** → usually plural: "Tom and Jerry **are** friends."
- **Or/Nor** → agrees with **closer** subject: "Neither the students nor the teacher **is** ready." / "Neither the teacher nor the students **are** ready."
- **As well as / along with / together with** → agrees with FIRST subject: "The teacher, as well as the students, **is** ready."

**Trap 6: Relative Clauses (who/which/that)**
- "He is one of the students who **are** excellent." (who refers to students)
- "He is the **only** one of the students who **is** excellent." (only one = singular)

**Trap 7: Quantifiers with "of"**
- "A number of students **are** here." (plural)
- "The number of students **is** large." (singular)
- "Half of the cake **is** gone." / "Half of the cookies **are** gone."

#### Practice (from Subject Verb Agreement PDF)
1. The collection of rare stamps **was** valued at $10,000.
2. Neither the manager nor the employees **were** satisfied.
3. Each of the participants **has** received a certificate.
4. A variety of options **are** available.

### 🔧 MODIFIERS: Placement and Logic (from 5_Modifiers.pdf, 5_(Ans) Modifiers.pdf)

#### Core Rule: A modifier must be placed next to what it modifies.

#### Types of Modifiers
| Type | Example | Modifies |
|------|---------|----------|
| **Adjective** | "The **red** car" | Noun (car) |
| **Adverb** | "He ran **quickly**" | Verb (ran) |
| **Participial Phrase** | "**Walking home**, I saw..." | Subject of main clause (I) |
| **Prepositional Phrase** | "The book **on the table**" | Noun (book) |
| **Appositive** | "My friend **, a doctor,** helped" | Noun (friend) |
| **Relative Clause** | "The man **who called**" | Noun (man) |

#### Dangling Modifiers (Most Tested!)
**Wrong**: "**Walking down the street**, the trees were beautiful."
- Who was walking? Not the trees!
**Right**: "**Walking down the street**, **I** saw beautiful trees."
- Or: "As **I walked** down the street, the trees were beautiful."

**SAT Pattern**: Introductory participial phrase (-ing/-ed) must modify the subject of the main clause.

| Dangling | Fixed |
|----------|-------|
| "Tired from the trip, the hotel room welcomed us." | "Tired from the trip, **we** found the hotel room welcoming." |
| "To pass the test, studying is necessary." | "To pass the test, **you must study**." / "**Studying** is necessary to pass the test." |

#### Misplaced Modifiers
**Wrong**: "She almost **drove** the car for ten hours." (Did she almost drive it, or drive it for almost ten hours?)
**Right**: "She drove the car for **almost** ten hours."

**Wrong**: "He only **eats** vegetables." (He does nothing else but eat vegetables?)
**Right**: "He eats **only** vegetables." (Vegetables are the only thing he eats.)

#### Modification Logic Traps
| Trap | Example | Fix |
|------|---------|-----|
| **Ambiguous reference** | "The organization helps families with children." | "The organization helps families **that have** children." |
| **Comparative modifier** | "Like his brother, the car is fast." | "Like his brother, **he** drives a fast car." / "His car, **like his brother's**, is fast." |
| **Which/That confusion** | "The book which is on the table..." | "That" for restrictive (essential), "which" for non-restrictive (extra info, needs commas) |

#### Practice (from Modifiers PDF)
1. **Running late**, the meeting started without her. → **Because she was running late**, the meeting started without her.
2. The researcher published a study **challenging** the theory. (Correct — participial phrase modifies "study")
3. **To qualify**, the application must be complete. (Correct — infinitive phrase modifies understood subject of main clause)

### 📍 PUNCTUATION MASTERY: Commas, Dashes, Colons, Semicolons (from 14_Commas, Dashes, Colons.pdf, 14_(Ans).pdf)

#### Commas: The Big Four Uses on SAT
| Use | Rule | Example |
|-----|------|---------|
| **1. List/Series** | Separate 3+ items (Oxford comma preferred) | "apples, oranges, and bananas" |
| **2. Compound Sentence** | FANBOYS + comma between independent clauses | "I like tea, **but** she prefers coffee." |
| **3. Introductory Element** | After dependent clause/phrase starting sentence | "**After the rain stopped**, we went out." |
| **4. Non-Essential (Non-Restrictive)** | Around extra info you could remove | "My brother**, who lives in NYC**, is visiting." |

**Comma Traps to Avoid:**
- **Don't** separate subject and verb: "The dog, barked." ✗
- **Don't** separate verb and object: "She ate, the apple." ✗
- **Don't** use between compound predicates (same subject): "He ran and jumped." (no comma)

#### Semicolons: Two Uses
| Use | Rule | Example |
|-----|------|---------|
| **1. Join Independent Clauses** | No conjunction; closely related ideas | "It rained; the game was canceled." |
| **2. Complex Lists** | Items already contain commas | "Cities: Paris, France; London, UK; Tokyo, Japan." |

**Semicolon + Transition**: "It rained; **therefore**, the game was canceled." (transition needs semicolon before, comma after)

#### Colons: Three Uses
| Use | Rule | Example |
|-----|------|---------|
| **1. After Independent Clause** | Introduces list, explanation, quote | "She brought three things: a pen, paper, and water." |
| **2. Between Independent Clauses** | Second explains/illustrates first | "He had one goal: win the championship." |
| **3. Formal/Conventional** | Time, ratios, salutations | "3:30 PM", "2:1", "Dear Sir:" |

**Colon Trap**: Must follow a **complete sentence** (independent clause).
- ✗ "Her favorite colors are: red and blue."
- ✓ "Her favorite colors are **red and blue**." (no colon)
- ✓ "She has two favorite colors: **red and blue**."

#### Dashes: Em Dashes (—) — Two Uses
| Use | Rule | Example |
|-----|------|---------|
| **1. Pair (like parentheses)** | Set off non-essential / emphatic info | "The winner—**my best friend**—celebrated." |
| **2. Single (like colon)** | After independent clause for emphasis/explanation | "She got the job—her dream since childhood." |

**Dash vs. Comma vs. Parentheses:**
- Commas: mild separation, flow continues
- Dashes: strong emphasis, dramatic pause, or contains commas
- Parentheses: whispered aside, truly incidental

#### Practice (from Commas, Dashes, Colons PDF)
1. The experiment failed—**the equipment was faulty**. (Single dash = explanation)
2. Three students—**Anna, Ben, and Cara**—won awards. (Pair of dashes = non-essential list)
3. She studied: **biology, chemistry, and physics**. (Colon after complete sentence → list)
4. It was raining; **however**, we played anyway. (Semicolon + transition)

### 🏗️ PARALLELISM: Structure and Logic

From Standard English Conventions 3 (10_W, 11_W).

#### Core Rule: Items in a list, comparison, or paired structure must have the **same grammatical form**.

#### Parallelism Patterns
| Structure | Wrong | Right |
|-----------|-------|-------|
| **List of verbs** | "She likes **reading, to write, and swimming**" | "She likes **reading, writing, and swimming**" |
| **List of infinitives** | "To **eat, sleeping, and to run**" | "To **eat, sleep, and run**" |
| **List of nouns** | "**His intelligence, kindness, and he is funny**" | "**His intelligence, kindness, and humor**" |
| **Correlative pairs** | "Not only **smart but also works hard**" | "Not only **smart but also hardworking**" |
| **Comparisons** | "The movie was **better than the book**" | "The movie was **better than the book was**" / "better than the book" (ellipsis OK if clear) |

#### Common SAT Parallelism Triggers
| Trigger | Pattern |
|---------|---------|
| **And / Or / But** | X and Y (same form) |
| **Not only... but also** | Not only X but also Y |
| **Either... or / Neither... nor** | Either X or Y |
| **Both... and** | Both X and Y |
| **Than / As** | More X than Y (parallel comparison) |
| **Rather than / Instead of** | X rather than Y |

#### Gerund vs. Infinitive Parallelism
- "She enjoys **swimming, hiking, and biking**." (all gerunds)
- "She wants **to swim, to hike, and to bike**." (all infinitives)
- "She wants **to swim, hike, and bike**." (OK: "to" distributes)

#### Practice
1. The job requires **patience, attention to detail, and being organized**.
   → "patience, attention to detail, and **organization**" (all nouns)

2. He decided **to stay home rather than going out**.
   → "to stay home rather than **go out**" (parallel infinitives) OR "**staying home rather than going out**" (parallel gerunds)

### 🔗 PRONOUNS: Agreement, Case, and Reference

#### Pronoun-Antecedent Agreement
| Antecedent | Pronoun |
|------------|---------|
| Singular noun | he/she/it, his/her/its |
| Plural noun | they, their, them |
| Indefinite (singular) | he or she, his or her |
| Collective (singular) | it, its |

**Singular "They"**: Now accepted for unknown/gender-neutral singular antecedents.
- "Each student should bring **their** own calculator." ✓ (modern usage)

#### Pronoun Case
| Role | Subject Case | Object Case | Possessive |
|------|--------------|-------------|------------|
| **Singular** | I, he, she, it, who | me, him, her, it, whom | my, his, her, its, whose |
| **Plural** | we, they, who | us, them, whom | our, their, whose |

**Test**: Isolate the pronoun.
- "Between you and **I**" → "Between **me**" → **Between you and me**.
- "She is taller than **I**" → "She is taller than **I am**" → **I** (subject of implied verb).

#### Ambiguous Pronoun Reference
- "The doctor told the patient that **he** needed surgery." (Who? Ambiguous)
- Fix: "The doctor told the patient, '**You** need surgery.'" OR "The doctor said **the patient** needed surgery."

#### Who vs. Whom
- **Who** = subject (he/she/they)
- **Whom** = object (him/her/them)
- Test: Replace with he/him. "To **who/whom** did you give it?" → "You gave it to **him**" → **whom**.

8. She only **eats** organic food. → She eats **only** organic food. (modifier placement)

**Punctuation:**
9. The recipe calls for three ingredients: **flour, sugar, and eggs**.
10. It was a difficult test; **nevertheless**, she passed.
11. The winner—**a complete surprise**—accepted the trophy.

**Parallelism:**
12. The course covers **reading, writing, and how to do math**.
    → "reading, writing, and **math**" (all nouns)
13. She likes **to swim, hiking, and to bike**.
    → "to swim, **hike, and bike**" OR "**swimming, hiking, and biking**"

**Pronouns:**
14. Each of the students must submit **their** application by Friday. (singular they OK)
15. The teacher gave the award to **whoever** earned it. (subject of "earned" → whoever)

### 💡 KEY STRATEGIES FOR NEW WRITING MATERIAL

1. **Tenses**: Find the time marker → determine relationship to other verbs → select consistent tense. Memorize the conditional sequences.

2. **Subject-Verb Agreement**: Cross out prepositional phrases. For compound subjects with or/nor, match the **closer** subject. "A number of" = plural, "The number of" = singular.

3. **Modifiers**: Introductory -ing/-ed phrases MUST modify the subject of the main clause. Check: Who is doing the action?

4. **Punctuation**: 
   - Semicolon = period (between independent clauses)
   - Colon = after complete sentence (introduces list/explanation)
   - Dashes = emphasis or non-essential with commas inside
   - Commas = lists, FANBOYS, introductory, non-essential

5. **Parallelism**: Circle the trigger (and, or, but, than, not only). Make both sides match grammatically.

6. **Conciseness**: Shorter is usually better IF it keeps the meaning. Watch for "due to the fact that," "in order to," passive voice.

7. **Process of Elimination**: On "which is best" questions, eliminate grammar errors first, then choose most concise correct version.
