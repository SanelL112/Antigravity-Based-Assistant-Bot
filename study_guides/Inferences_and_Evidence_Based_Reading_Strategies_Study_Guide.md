## 🎯 The "Must Be True" Inference Framework

### The Golden Rule of SAT Inferences
> **An inference on the SAT is NOT a guess, a possibility, or a reasonable conclusion.**
> **An inference is a statement that MUST BE TRUE based ONLY on the passage.**

### The "Must Be True" Test
For every answer choice, ask: **"Does this HAVE to be true based on the passage alone?"**
- **Yes** → Correct answer
- **Could be false** → Eliminate
- **Requires outside knowledge** → Eliminate
- **Overstates/understates** → Eliminate

### Inference vs. Assumption vs. Implication
| Concept | Definition | SAT Status |
|---------|------------|------------|
| **Inference** | Logically necessary conclusion from text | ✅ Tested |
| **Assumption** | Unstated premise author relies on | ⚠️ Rarely tested directly |
| **Implication** | What author suggests but doesn't state | ✅ Tested (as "author implies") |
| **Speculation** | What *might* be true | ❌ Never correct |

## 📋 Inference Types and Question Stems

### 1. Logical Deduction (Chaining Statements)
**Question Stems:**
- "Which can be logically inferred?"
- "It follows from the passage that..."
- "Based on the passage, which must be true?"

**Strategy:** Chain 2+ explicit statements.
```
Passage: "All A are B. X is A."
Inference: "X is B." (Must be true)
```

**Practice:**
> **Passage:** "Every student who passed the exam studied for at least 20 hours. Marcus passed the exam."
> **Valid Inference:** Marcus studied for at least 20 hours.
> **Invalid:** Marcus is a good student. (Not in passage)

---

### 2. Author Implication (Tone/Word Choice)
**Question Stems:**
- "The author implies/suggests that..."
- "The passage suggests..."
- "It can be inferred that the author believes..."

**Strategy:** Look for:
- **Qualifiers** ("surprisingly," "remarkably," "concerningly")
- **Contrast markers** ("however," "unexpectedly," "despite")
- **Evaluative adjectives** ("flawed," "robust," "preliminary," "definitive")

**Example:**
> **Passage:** "The study's sample size was *only* 15 participants, *yet* the authors drew *broad* conclusions."
> **Valid Inference:** The author believes the study's conclusions are not well-supported.
> **Invalid:** The study is wrong. (Too strong — "not well-supported" ≠ "wrong")

---

### 3. Quantitative Inference (Data-Based)
**Question Stems:**
- "Based on the data in the passage/table/graph..."
- "Which statement is supported by the data?"

**Strategy:** Calculate or compare ONLY from given numbers.
- **Do:** Percentages, ratios, differences, trends shown
- **Don't:** Project beyond data, assume causation from correlation

**Example:**
> **Passage:** "In 2020, 40% of households had streaming. In 2023, 65%."
> **Valid Inference:** Streaming household penetration increased by 25 percentage points.
> **Invalid:** Streaming will reach 90% by 2026. (Projection)

---

### 4. Comparative Inference
**Question Stems:**
- "Unlike X, Y..." / "In contrast to X, Y..."
- "The passage suggests X differs from Y in that..."

**Strategy:** Find explicit contrast in passage. Inference = the *flip side* of stated contrast.

**Example:**
> **Passage:** "Traditional lectures result in 20% retention. Active learning yields 75% retention."
> **Valid Inference:** Active learning produces higher retention than traditional lectures.
> **Invalid:** Traditional lectures are ineffective. (20% ≠ ineffective)

---

### 5. Causal/Mechanistic Inference
**Question Stems:**
- "The passage suggests that X causes Y by..."
- "The mechanism described implies that..."

**Strategy:** Identify the **explicit mechanism** described. Inference = logical consequence of that mechanism.

**Example:**
> **Passage:** "The drug inhibits Enzyme X, which normally breaks down Neurotransmitter Y. Higher Y levels reduce anxiety."
> **Valid Inference:** Patients taking the drug would have higher Neurotransmitter Y levels.
> **Invalid:** The drug cures anxiety. (Mechanism ≠ proven clinical outcome)

## 📊 Evidence-Based Reading: Command of Evidence

### Two Question Formats

#### Format A: "Which choice provides the best evidence for the answer to the previous question?"
- **Paired with** a preceding question (usually Inference, Central Idea, or Author's Purpose)
- **Strategy:** Answer previous question FIRST, then find the lines that *directly support* your answer

#### Format B: "Which choice best supports the claim that [claim]?"
- **Standalone** — given a claim, find evidence
- **Strategy:** Treat claim as question, find lines that answer it

### Evidence Evaluation Checklist
For each evidence choice, ask:
1. **Direct?** Does it *directly* address the claim/question? (Not tangentially)
2. **Sufficient?** Does it *alone* support the claim? (Or need other sentences?)
3. **Accurate?** Does it match the passage meaning? (Not distorted)
4. **Scope?** Is it the right *level* of generality? (Not too broad/narrow)

### Evidence Types Ranked by Strength
| Rank | Type | Example |
|------|------|---------|
| 1️⃣ | **Direct statement** | "The author states: 'X is true.'" |
| 2️⃣ | **Data/statistic** | "65% of participants showed improvement." |
| 3️⃣ | **Expert testimony** | "Dr. Smith, lead researcher, concludes..." |
| 4️⃣ | **Concrete example** | "In the 2019 trial, patients..." |
| 5️⃣ | **Logical reasoning** | "Since A causes B, and B causes C..." |
| 6️⃣ | **Analogy/comparison** | "Like the 1990 case, this situation..." |

## 📈 Quantitative Evidence (Charts, Graphs, Tables)

### DSAT Quantitative Evidence Question Types

#### 1. Direct Read
"According to the graph, what was the value in 2020?"
- **Strategy:** Locate point, read axis, match units

#### 2. Comparison
"Which group showed the largest increase?"
- **Strategy:** Calculate/compare changes for each category

#### 3. Trend Identification
"What trend does the data show from 2010–2020?"
- **Strategy:** Overall direction (up/down/flat/cyclical), not year-by-year

#### 4. Support/Contradict Claim
"The author claims X. Does the graph support this?"
- **Strategy:** Evaluate claim against *actual* data, not general impression

#### 5. Inference from Data
"Based on the table, which can be inferred?"
- **Strategy:** Apply "Must Be True" test to data-derived statements

### Common Graph/Table Types on DSAT
| Type | Key Features | What to Check |
|------|--------------|---------------|
| **Line graph** | Trends over time | Axes labels, units, multiple lines |
| **Bar chart** | Comparisons across categories | Grouped vs stacked, baseline |
| **Scatterplot** | Correlation | Direction, strength, outliers, line of fit |
| **Table** | Exact values | Row/column headers, totals, percentages |
| **Histogram** | Distribution | Bins, frequency, skew, center |

### Quantitative Trap Answers
| Trap | Example | Defense |
|------|---------|---------|
| **Wrong units** | Graph in millions, answer in thousands | Circle units on axes |
| **Percent vs percentage points** | "Increased 5%" vs "increased 5 percentage points" | Calculate: 10% → 15% = 5 pp, 50% increase |
| **Correlation = causation** | "X and Y rise together, so X causes Y" | Never infer causation from correlational data |
| **Cherry-picked timeframe** | "Increased since 2015" (but fell 2010–2015) | Look at full range |
| **Absolute vs relative** | "Doubled!" (from 2 to 4 cases) | Check base rate |

## 🔗 Best Evidence Paired Questions

### The Paired Question Structure
**Question 1:** Inference / Central Idea / Purpose / Function
**Question 2:** "Which choice provides the best evidence for the answer to the previous question?"

### Optimal Strategy: Answer Q1 FIRST, Then Q2
**Do NOT read Q2 first.** It biases you toward evidence that fits a wrong Q1 answer.

#### Step-by-Step:
1. **Read Q1** — determine question type
2. **Answer Q1** — find answer in passage, formulate in your words
3. **Locate support** — find the *specific lines* that prove your Q1 answer
4. **Read Q2 choices** — match to your located lines
5. **Verify** — does this evidence *alone* support Q1 answer?

### Q1 Answer → Q2 Evidence Mapping
| Q1 Type | What to Find for Q2 |
|---------|---------------------|
| **Inference** | The 2+ statements you chained |
| **Central Idea** | Thesis statement + key supporting sentence |
| **Author's Purpose** | The "why" signal (e.g., "to argue," "to explain") |
| **Function** | The structural role marker (e.g., "for example," "however") |
| **Vocab in Context** | The context clues around the word |
| **Author's View** | Evaluative language / stance markers |

### When Stuck on Q1: Use Q2 to Backsolve
If Q1 is ambiguous:
1. Read Q2 choices
2. For each, ask: "If this is the evidence, what would Q1's answer be?"
3. See which Q1 answer is *best supported* by a Q2 choice
4. **Risk:** Only use when truly stuck — primary strategy is Q1 first

## 🎓 Advanced Inference Strategies

### 1. The "Two-Sentence Chain" Technique
Most logical deduction inferences require chaining **exactly two sentences**.
- Find Sentence A (premise 1)
- Find Sentence B (premise 2)
- Chain: A + B → Inference

**Practice:** Number sentences in passage. Draw arrows between chained ones.

### 2. The "Scope Filter"
Every inference has a **scope** (topic, group, timeframe, condition).
- **Correct inference:** Same or narrower scope than evidence
- **Trap inference:** Broader scope

**Example:**
> Evidence: "Urban trees in Seattle reduce summer temperatures by 2°C."
> ✅ Valid: "Seattle's urban trees provide local cooling."
> ❌ Trap: "Trees solve global warming." (Scope explosion)

### 3. The "Qualifier Preservation" Rule
If evidence says "may," "suggests," "preliminary," "some" — inference **must preserve** that qualifier.
| Evidence Qualifier | Valid Inference | Trap Inference |
|-------------------|-----------------|----------------|
| "may reduce" | "could reduce" | "reduces" |
| "some studies show" | "there is some evidence" | "it is proven" |
| "in this sample" | "in this sample" | "in general" |
| "correlates with" | "is associated with" | "causes" |

### 4. The "Negative Inference" (What's NOT Said)
Sometimes inference = **absence of expected information**.
- Passage discusses benefits of X extensively, never mentions cost
- Inference: "The passage does not address the cost of X."
- **Only valid if** the absence is notable given the passage's scope

### 5. Comparative Passage Inferences
For paired passages:
- **Inference about Passage 1:** Use only Passage 1
- **Inference about Passage 2:** Use only Passage 2
- **Inference about relationship:** Use ICC method (Support/Contradict/Refine/Apply/Contextualize)
- **NEVER** mix evidence across passages for single-passage inferences

## ⚠️ Common Trap Answers (The "Deadly Dozen")

### 1. **Extreme Language**
- "Always," "never," "all," "none," "completely," "totally," "definitely"
- SAT passages are nuanced; extreme answers are almost always wrong

### 2. **Scope Expansion**
- Passage: "In this study of 500 students..."
- Trap: "Students generally..." / "Education research shows..."
- **Rule:** Inference scope ≤ Evidence scope

### 3. **Reversal**
- Passage: "X prevents Y."
- Trap: "Y prevents X." / "X causes Y."
- **Check:** Subject/object, cause/effect direction

### 4. **Unsupported Magnitude**
- Passage: "Slightly improved outcomes."
- Trap: "Dramatically improved" / "Significantly improved"
- **Rule:** Preserve the passage's degree language

### 5. **Outside Knowledge**
- Requires biology/history/economics knowledge not in passage
- **Defense:** "Can I prove this using ONLY the passage?"

### 6. **Half-Right / Half-Wrong**
- "X is true AND Y is true" — one part correct, one wrong
- **Rule:** Entire answer choice must be correct

### 7. **Distortion**
- Twists passage meaning subtly
- Passage: "The policy *failed to achieve* its goals."
- Trap: "The policy *caused harm*." (Failure ≠ harm)

### 8. **True But Irrelevant**
- Statement is factually correct (from passage) but doesn't answer the question
- **Check:** Does this *directly* support the specific inference asked?

### 9. **Conflation**
- Merges two distinct things from passage
- Passage: "Method A is fast. Method B is accurate."
- Trap: "Method A is fast and accurate."

### 10. **Temporal Shift**
- Passage: "Historically, X was believed."
- Trap: "X is believed." (Past vs present)

### 11. **Conditional as Absolute**
- Passage: "If funding continues, the project will expand."
- Trap: "The project will expand." (Ignores condition)

### 12. **Analogy as Evidence**
- Passage uses analogy to explain
- Trap treats analogy as proof
- Passage: "The cell membrane *functions like* a gate."
- Trap: "The cell membrane *is* a gate."

## 🎯 Practice Problems with Solutions

### Inference Set (from Inferences_L2, Inferences_L3)

#### Problem 1: Logical Deduction
**Passage:** "All participants who completed the 12-week program reported improved sleep quality. Maria completed the program."
**Question:** What can be logically inferred?
- A) Maria's sleep quality improved. ✅
- B) Maria has insomnia. ❌ (not in passage)
- C) The program cures sleep disorders. ❌ (overstates)
- D) All participants have better sleep than non-participants. ❌ (no comparison group)

**Answer:** A — Direct chain: All completers → improved. Maria → completer. Therefore Maria → improved.

---

#### Problem 2: Author Implication
**Passage:** "The new highway *ostensibly* reduces congestion, *yet* traffic data shows a 15% *increase* in peak-hour volume since opening."
**Question:** The author implies that:
- A) The highway failed to reduce congestion. ✅
- B) Traffic data is unreliable. ❌ (author cites it)
- C) Congestion is unsolvable. ❌ (overstates)
- D) The highway should be closed. ❌ (not implied)

**Answer:** A — "Ostensibly" (supposedly) + "yet" (contrast) + data showing increase = author believes claim is false.

---

#### Problem 3: Quantitative Inference
**Passage:** "In 2015, 30% of adults used Service X. By 2020, this rose to 45%. Among teens, usage grew from 60% to 85%."
**Question:** Based on the data, which must be true?
- A) Service X is more popular with teens than adults. ✅ (85% > 45% in 2020; 60% > 30% in 2015)
- B) Adult usage will surpass teen usage by 2030. ❌ (projection)
- C) Teens adopt technology faster than adults. ❌ (causal claim, not in data)
- D) Service X had 15% growth overall. ❌ (can't combine different populations)

**Answer:** A — Direct comparison of stated percentages. Same metric, same time points.

---

#### Problem 4: Comparative Inference
**Passage:** "Unlike the control group, which showed no change in inflammation markers, the treatment group exhibited a 40% reduction in CRP levels."
**Question:** The passage suggests that the control group:
- A) Had higher CRP levels than the treatment group at the end. ✅
- B) Had no inflammation. ❌ (no change ≠ no inflammation)
- C) Received a placebo. ❌ (not stated)
- D) Was smaller than the treatment group. ❌ (not stated)

**Answer:** A — Treatment reduced 40%, control no change → treatment end CRP < control end CRP.

---

#### Problem 5: Causal Inference
**Passage:** "The compound binds to Receptor R, blocking Inflammatory Signal S. Signal S normally triggers Cytokine C release. Elevated Cytokine C correlates with symptom severity."
**Question:** The passage suggests that the compound reduces symptoms by:
- A) Binding to Cytokine C directly. ❌ (binds Receptor R)
- B) Preventing Signal S from triggering Cytokine C release. ✅
- C) Reducing Receptor R production. ❌ (blocks, doesn't reduce production)
- D) Eliminating Cytokine C entirely. ❌ (blocks release, not eliminate)

**Answer:** B — Chain: Compound → blocks R → blocks S → prevents C release → reduces symptoms.

### Evidence-Based Reading Set

#### Problem 6: Best Evidence (Paired)
**Q1:** The author's attitude toward the proposed regulation is best described as:
**Passage excerpt:** "The regulation, while well-intentioned, imposes uniform standards on wildly diverse industries. Small manufacturers, lacking compliance infrastructure, face disproportionate burden. The one-size-fits-all approach ignores the very flexibility that drives innovation."

**Q2:** Which choice provides the best evidence for the answer to the previous question?
- A) "The regulation... imposes uniform standards..." ✅
- B) "Small manufacturers... face disproportionate burden." (Supports specific claim, not overall attitude)
- C) "The one-size-fits-all approach ignores..." (Supporting detail)
- D) "drives innovation" (Fragment)

**Q1 Answer:** Critical / Skeptical
**Q2 Answer:** A — Captures the overall evaluative stance ("well-intentioned" + criticism)

---

#### Problem 7: Standalone Evidence
**Claim:** "The study's methodology limits the generalizability of its findings."
**Passage:** "The sample consisted of 50 undergraduate psychology students at a single university. All participants were aged 18–22 and received course credit. The task was administered in a laboratory setting."
**Which choice best supports the claim?**
- A) "The sample consisted of 50 undergraduate psychology students..." ✅
- B) "All participants were aged 18–22..." (Partial — only age)
- C) "received course credit" (Only motivation)
- D) "administered in a laboratory setting" (Only setting)

**Answer:** A — Most comprehensive: narrow demographic + single institution + small N + volunteer bias + artificial setting

---

#### Problem 8: Quantitative Evidence
**Graph:** Bar chart showing "Customer Satisfaction by Support Channel"
- Phone: 72%
- Email: 65%
- Chat: 78%
- Social Media: 55%
- In-Person: 85%

**Claim:** "Customers prefer human interaction for support."
**Does the graph support this claim?**
- In-Person (human) = 85% ✓
- Phone (human) = 72% ✓
- Chat (often bot/hybrid) = 78% ✗ (high but not necessarily human)
- Email (human but async) = 65%
- Social Media (mixed) = 55%

**Answer:** Partially — In-Person and Phone support it; Chat complicates it. Best answer: "The data partially supports the claim: human channels (in-person, phone) score highest, but chat outperforms phone."

## ⏱️ Time Management Strategy

### Inference Questions: Target 75–90 seconds
| Phase | Time | Action |
|-------|------|--------|
| **Read question** | 5 sec | Identify inference type |
| **Locate evidence** | 20 sec | Find relevant sentences (skim for keywords) |
| **Chain/Analyze** | 25 sec | Apply Must Be True test |
| **Evaluate choices** | 20 sec | Eliminate traps, select must-be-true |
| **Verify** | 5 sec | Re-read choice + evidence |

### Command of Evidence (Paired): Target 2 min for pair
| Phase | Time | Action |
|-------|------|--------|
| **Q1: Answer** | 60 sec | Full inference process |
| **Q1: Locate support** | 15 sec | Mark lines |
| **Q2: Match** | 30 sec | Match choices to marked lines |
| **Verify pair** | 15 sec | Confirm Q2 evidence → Q1 answer |

### Command of Evidence (Standalone): Target 60–75 sec
| Phase | Time | Action |
|-------|------|--------|
| **Read claim** | 5 sec | Identify what needs support |
| **Scan passage** | 25 sec | Find relevant evidence |
| **Match choices** | 25 sec | Directness, sufficiency, accuracy |
| **Verify** | 10 sec | Does this choice alone support claim? |

### Quantitative Evidence: Target 60–90 sec
| Phase | Time | Action |
|-------|------|--------|
| **Read question + claim** | 10 sec | Know what to look for |
| **Orient to graphic** | 15 sec | Axes, units, labels, legend |
| **Extract data** | 20 sec | Read values, calculate if needed |
| **Evaluate** | 20 sec | Match to claim/choices |
| **Watch units** | 5 sec | Million vs thousand, % vs pp |

## 📌 Quick Reference: Inference Decision Tree

```
START: "What can be inferred?"
│
├─► Is it a LOGICAL CHAIN? (All A are B, X is A)
│    │   → Chain the explicit statements
│    │   → Must Be True = direct deduction
│    │
├─► Is it AUTHOR IMPLICATION? (tone, qualifiers, contrast)
│    │   → Find evaluative language
│    │   → Inference = what author suggests but doesn't state
│    │
├─► Is it QUANTITATIVE? (data, graph, table)
│    │   → Calculate/compare ONLY from given numbers
│    │   → Preserve units, qualifiers, scope
│    │
├─► Is it COMPARATIVE? (Unlike X, Y...)
│    │   → Find explicit contrast
│    │   → Inference = flip side of stated difference
│    │
└─► Is it CAUSAL/MECHANISTIC? (X blocks Y which triggers Z)
     │   → Trace the mechanism step by step
     │   → Inference = necessary consequence of mechanism
```

## 🎯 Elimination Checklist for Every Inference Choice

- [ ] **Must Be True?** (Not could be true, likely true, reasonable)
- [ ] **Passage Only?** (Zero outside knowledge required)
- [ ] **Scope Match?** (Inference scope ≤ Evidence scope)
- [ ] **Qualifiers Preserved?** (May → may; some → some; correlation → correlation)
- [ ] **Direction Correct?** (Cause/effect, subject/object not flipped)
- [ ] **No Distortion?** (Passage meaning not twisted)
- [ ] **Not Half-Wrong?** (Every clause in choice is correct)
- [ ] **Answers the Specific Question?** (Not just true, but responsive)
