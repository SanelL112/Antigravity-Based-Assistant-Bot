## Percent Versions

### The 3 Versions (from classroom notes):

| Version | Question | Convert | Multiply By |
|---------|----------|---------|-------------|
| **Percent OF** | "What is 20% of 80?" | p/100 | 0.20 × 80 = 16 |
| **Percent MORE** | "Increase 80 by 20%" | 1 + p/100 | 1.20 × 80 = 96 |
| **Percent LESS** | "Decrease 80 by 20%" | 1 - p/100 | 0.80 × 80 = 64 |

### Percent Conversion:
- 20% → 20/100 = **0.20** or **1/5**
- 75% → 75/100 = **0.75** or **3/4**
- 125% → 125/100 = **1.25** or **5/4**

## Finding Percents

### Type 1: Part / Whole (Basic)
$$\text{What percent} = \frac{\text{Part}}{\text{Whole}} \times 100$$

**Example:** 16 is what percent of 42? → 16/42 × 100 = **38.1%**

### Type 2: Percent Change
$$\frac{\text{New} - \text{Old}}{\text{Old}} \times 100$$

**CRITICAL:** The "Old" or "standard" is the denominator ALWAYS.

### Type 3: Comparative Language

| Question | Formula | Standard Is |
|----------|---------|-------------|
| "A is what % OF B?" | (A/B) × 100 | B |
| "A is what % GREATER than B?" | ((A-B)/B) × 100 | B |
| "B is what % LESS than A?" | ((A-B)/A) × 100 | A |

**The key:** The standard is ALWAYS the thing AFTER "than" or the thing after "of."

**Example:** Ashish = 42, Aashni = 16
- Aashni is what % less than Ashish? → (42-16)/42 × 100 = **61.9%** ← Ashish is standard
- Ashish is what % older than Aashni? → (42-16)/16 × 100 = **162.5%** ← Aashni is standard

## Sequential Percent Changes

When multiple percent changes happen in sequence, **MULTIPLY the factors**.

**Example 1:** 20% discount then 10% tax
- Factors: 0.80 × 1.10 = 0.88
- Net effect: 12% DECREASE from original

**Example 2:** 25% increase followed by 20% decrease
- Factors: 1.25 × 0.80 = 1.00
- Net effect: NO CHANGE!

**Example 3:** 20% increase then 20% decrease
- Factors: 1.20 × 0.80 = 0.96
- Net effect: 4% DECREASE (not zero!)

**⚠ KEY INSIGHT:** Equal % up then down = small DECREASE. The decrease always "wins" slightly.

## Reverse Percent Problems

**Problem:** "After a 20% discount and 10% tax, the price is $88. What was the original?"

**Solution:** Work backwards by dividing by each factor.
$$\text{Original} = \frac{\$88}{(0.80)(1.10)} = \frac{\$88}{0.88} = \$100$$

## Factor of Change Approach

### Framework (from classroom):

When told how certain quantities change and asked about a dependent quantity:

1. **Start with the formula** (given or known)
2. **Eliminate constants** (coefficients stay fixed)
3. **Replace each variable** with its factor of change
4. **Multiply out** to find the overall factor
5. **Convert to percent change**: (factor - 1) × 100 = % increase; (1 - factor) × 100 = % decrease

### Example Problems:

**Kinetic Energy:** KE = ½mv²
- Mass decreases 20% → m becomes 0.80m
- Speed increases 20% → v becomes 1.20v
- KE_new = ½(0.80m)(1.20v)² = ½mv² × 0.80 × 1.44 = KE_original × 1.152
- → **15.2% INCREASE**

**Circle Area:** A = πr²
- r increases 25% → 1.25r
- A_new = π(1.25r)² = πr² × 1.5625
- → **56.25% INCREASE** (note: doubling r quadruples A!)

**Volume of Cylinder:** V = πr²h
- r decreases 10% → 0.90r, h constant
- V_new = π(0.90r)²h = πr²h × 0.81
- → **19% DECREASE** (not 10%!)

## Ratios & Proportions

### Ratios Give Proportional Relationships, NOT Exact values.

IfBoys:Girls = 2:3, then:
- Boys = 2m (unknown multiple m)
- Girls = 3m
- Total = 5m
- Difference = m

**You CANNOT determine the actual numbers** without more information (like total count or difference).

### Example:
Boys:Girls = 2:3, Area of rectangle = 200 with width:length = 5:2.
- Width = 5m, Length = 2m
- Area = 5m × 2m = 10m² = 200
- m² = 20, m = √20 = 2√5

### Example 2:
Width:Length = 5:2, Area = 200. Find perimeter.
- Let width = 5m, length = 2m
- Area = 5m × 2m = 10m² = 200 → m² = 20 → m = √20
- Perimeter = 2(5m + 2m) = 14m = 14√20 = 28√5

### SAT Trap:
Don't confuse "ratio of 5:2" with "difference of 3." The actual values are 5m and 2m, not 5 and 2!

## Rate Problems

### Rate Formula:
$$\text{Rate} = \frac{\text{Quantity}}{\text{Consumption}}$$

**Common rate = Speed:**
$$\text{Speed} = \frac{\text{Distance}}{\text{Time}} → D = S × T$$

### Average Speed (Critical Formula):
⚠️ Average speed is NOT the average of speeds (unless times are equal).

$$\text{Average Speed} = \frac{\text{Total Distance}}{\text{Total Time}}$$

**Round-trip Example:** Drive to city at 40 mph, return at 60 mph. Average speed?
- Total distance = 2x (x each way)
- Time to city = x/40, Time back = x/60
- Total time = x/40 + x/60 = (3x+2x)/120 = 5x/120 = x/24
- Average speed = 2x/(x/24) = **48 mph** (NOT 50!)

### Organizing Rate Problems (Chart Method):
| Trip | Distance | Speed | Time |
|------|----------|-------|------|
| To City | x | 40 | x/40 |
| Return | x | 60 | x/60 |
| Total | 2x | — | x/24 |

## Mixture Problems

### General Formula:
$$V_1 × c_1 + V_2 × c_2 = (V_1 + V_2) × c_{\text{final}}$$

Where V = volume, c = concentration (%)

### Example:
"How many liters of 25% acid must be added to 40L of 40% acid to make a 30% solution?"

| | Volume | Amount Acid |
|---|--------|------------|
| Mix 1 | x | 0.25x |
| Mix 2 | 40 | 0.40(40) = 16 |
| Final | x + 40 | 0.30(x + 40) |

Equation: 0.25x + 16 = 0.30(x + 40)
→ 0.25x + 16 = 0.30x + 12
→ 4 = 0.05x
→ x = **80 liters**

## Common SAT Traps

1. **Confusing "percent OF" with "percent GREATER THAN"** — the standard changes
2. **Adding sequential % changes** instead of multiplying factors
3. **Thinking ratios give absolute values** — they give proportional values only (need the multiplier m)
4. **Calculating average speed as average of speeds** — use total distance / total time
5. **Ignoring that area/volume scale by the factor squared/cubed** — a 10% radius increase → 21% area increase
6. **Wrong direction in reverse percents** — divide by factors, don't multiply
7. **Mistaking "difference" in ratios for the actual values** — ratio 2:3 means 2m and 3m, difference is m, not 1
