## What Are Transitions?

A **transition** is a word, phrase, or punctuation mark that shows the **logical relationship** between one idea and the next. The SAT tests whether you can identify which transition accurately connects two parts of a text.

---

## The 8 Transition Categories

### Category 1: OPPOSITION / CONTRAST
**Function:** Presents an opposing or limiting point of view.

**Keywords:** however, on the other hand, conversely, whereas, while, in contrast, nevertheless, yet, on the other hand, unfortunately, although, even though, though

**How to spot it:** The second sentence contradicts, limits, or qualifies the first sentence.

**Example:** "Final Fantasy is often considered the greatest video game franchise. **However**, there are many that deem the franchise overrated."

---

### Category 2: EVIDENCE / ILLUSTRATION
**Function:** Gives a specific example or piece of evidence to support a claim.

**Keywords:** indeed, as a matter of fact, in fact, to illustrate, for instance, for example

**How to spot it:** A specific example follows a more general statement.

**Example:** "Biology is an amazing scientific subject. **Indeed**, there are many firsthand applications, such as in the field of forensic sciences."

---

### Category 3: ADDITION
**Function:** Adds another supporting point or piece of evidence.

**Keywords:** furthermore, in addition, moreover, also, too, as well, additionally, not to mention

**How to spot it:** The new information supports and adds to the previous idea (does NOT change direction).

**Example:** "Biology blends the best applications of many disciplines. **Furthermore**, it is one of the most employable degrees."

---

### Category 4: CAUSE & EFFECT
**Function:** Shows a consequence or result.

**Keywords:** consequently, as a result, because, hence, therefore, thus, accordingly, so, for this reason

**How to spot it:** What follows is the logical outcome or conclusion of what came before.

**Example:** "The Backrooms is a movie devoid of any logic. **Therefore**, the movie is horrible."

---

### Category 5: CONCESSION
**Function:** Acknowledges a partial truth, then supersedes it with a stronger point.

**Keywords:** granted, nevertheless, although, regardless, despite, even if, nonetheless, still, even so

**How to spot it:** "Yes, X is true, BUT here's the thing that matters more..."

**Example:** "Although Matias has no desire to study, Ashish plans to lock him in a cage with so many factoring exercises that he has no option but to complete them."

---

### Category 6: SEQUENCING / ORDER
**Function:** Shows chronological or logical order of events/actions.

**Keywords:** finally, subsequently, previously, at first, next, then, eventually, before, toward, afterward

**How to spot it:** Time words or step-by-step progression.

**Example:** "Divyang skipped his SAT class to play basketball. **Next**, he walked outside to see that his entire wardrobe had been stolen."

---

### Category 7: SIMILARITY
**Function:** Shows that two things share a constant theme or trait.

**Keywords:** in the same way, similarly, likewise, by the same token

**How to spot it:** A parallel structure — "Just as X, so too Y" or "X is... Similarly, Y is..."

**Example:** "Goldfish is the best snack for boring lectures. **Likewise**, popcorn is best for watching exciting movies."

---

### Category 8: SUMMARIZE / RESTATE
**Function:** Concludes or restates the main idea.

**Keywords:** in conclusion, in summary, to sum up, in short, in other words

**How to spot it:** Brings closure or says the same thing more concisely.

## The ICC Process (3-Step)

When you encounter a transition question on the SAT:

### Step I — IDENTIFY
- Read the sentence(s) BEFORE the transition
- Determine what CLAIM, POINT, or IDEA is being expressed
- What is the function of this first part?

### Step C — CLASSIFY
- Read the sentence AFTER the transition
- What is its relationship to the prior sentence?
- Is it: contrasting? supporting? adding? showing consequence? sequencing? similar? conceding? summarizing?

### Step C — CHOOSE
- Select the transition word/phrase that matches the classified relationship
- Verify by reading the full passage with your chosen transition in place
- Make sure it flows naturally and logically

## Reading Methodology: Annotation & Foreshadowing

### Step 1: Annotate Transitions
On every passage, actively underline or circle:
- Transition words (but, however, furthermore, yet, moreover, although, thus, therefore, etc.)
- Transitional punctuation (semicolons joining ICs, dashes, colons)

### Step 2: Focus AFTER the Transition
The content that comes AFTER the transition is where the argument moves. Use it to predict.

### Step 3: Foreshadowing
Based on the transition and what follows, **predict what will come next** in the passage:
- If "however" → the author will challenge the previous point
- If "for example" → specific evidence is coming
- If "therefore" → a conclusion is being drawn

### Step 4: Confirm or Deny
As you keep reading:
- **Confirmed** → you understood the argument; keep going
- **Denied** → you misread; STOP, reassess, restart with the correct understanding

## Contrast vs. Unanimous

When comparing two passages or two parts of one passage:

| Contrast | Unanimous |
|----------|-----------|
| Authors DISAGREE or emphasize DIFFERENT factors | Authors AGREE or emphasize the SAME factor |
| Critical for understanding complexity | Strengthens a claim by convergence |
| Ask: "What factor did one miss?" | Ask: "What shared conclusion emerges?" |

**Why it matters:** If you mistake a contrast for being unanimous (or vice versa), you'll get cross-text questions wrong completely.

## Practice Questions with Explanations

**Q1:** "In hindsight, given the ideas about the natural world circulating among British scientists in the 1800s, the theory of natural selection was an obvious next step. It may not have been a coincidence, _______ that Charles Darwin and Alfred Wallace arrived at the concept independently."

Answer: **D (for example)**
Explanation: The second sentence gives a SPECIFIC ILLUSTRATION of how the obvious-next-step idea manifests. The "Independent discovery" example supports why natural selection was inevitable. This is Evidence/Illustration, so "for example" fits perfectly.

**Q2:** "In his essay 'Of Coaches,' Montaigne explores light subjects. _______ his personal motto was 'What do I know?'"

Answer: **C (Fittingly)**
Explanation: His motto "What do I know?" FITS his described behavior of questioning perceptions. The relationship is one of fit/agreement/congruence.

**Q3:** "Blanche K. Bruce was an outspoken abolitionist... It was relatively rare for supporters to be secretive. _______ they were vocal abolitionists like Bruce."

Answer: **C (For example)** or **A (Fittingly)**
Explanation: Bruce is given as a SPECIFIC INSTANCE of vocal abolitionist behavior. This is illustration/example.

**Q4:** "Levygotsky described ZPD. A learner completes a task with guidance; _______ the learner internalizes the skill."

Answer: **D (subsequently)**
Explanation: SEQUENCING — first with guidance, THEN internalizes. Temporal/logical progression.

**Q5:** "For decades, heat always diffuses hot→cold. _______ researchers found heat moving as a wave."

Answer: **B (However)**
Explanation: CONTRAST — the new finding CHALLENGES the long-held assumption.

**Q6:** "William Graham Sumner defined folkways. _______ he also identified mores."

Answer: **D (Moreover)**
Explanation: ADDITION — he identified one thing, AND ANOTHER category. Both are aspects of his theory.

## 📌 Key Mnemonic

**Remember: "FIRE-CAST"**
- **F**it/Fittingly
- **I**llustrate/Indeed (Evidence)
- **R**esist/However (Contrast)
- **E**xtend/Furthermore (Addition)
- **C**onclude/Consequently (Causal)
- **A**gree/Similarly (Similarity)
- **S**equence/Subsequently (Order)
- **T**hough/Granted (Concession)
