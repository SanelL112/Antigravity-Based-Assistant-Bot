### 1. Calculus: Limits and Continuity

#### Key Concepts and Definitions
- **Limit**: The value that a function approaches as the input approaches some value.
- **Continuity**: A function is continuous at a point if the limit of the function at that point equals the value of the function at that point.
- **Limit Laws**: Rules for combining limits, such as the sum, product, and quotient laws.
- **Squeeze Theorem**: A theorem that helps determine the limit of a function by comparing it to two other functions whose limits are known.

#### Important Formulas or Principles
- **Limit Laws**:
  - \(\lim_{x \to a} [f(x) + g(x)] = \lim_{x \to a} f(x) + \lim_{x \to a} g(x)\)
  - \(\lim_{x \to a} [f(x) \cdot g(x)] = \lim_{x \to a} f(x) \cdot \lim_{x \to a} g(x)\)
  - \(\lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \frac{\lim_{x \to a} f(x)}{\lim_{x \to a} g(x)}\) (provided \(\lim_{x \to a} g(x) \neq 0\))
- **Squeeze Theorem**: If \(f(x) \leq g(x) \leq h(x)\) for all \(x\) in an open interval containing \(a\), and \(\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = \lim_{x \to a} h(x) = L\), then \(\lim_{x \to a} g(x) = L\).

#### Common Mistakes to Avoid
- **Incorrect Application of Limit Laws**: Forgetting to check the conditions under which the limit laws apply.
- **Misapplying the Squeeze Theorem**: Using functions that do not properly "squeeze" the target function.
- **Assuming Continuity Without Verification**: Assuming a function is continuous at a point without checking the limit and function value.

#### Practice Problem Types
1. **Evaluate Limits**: Calculate \(\lim_{x \to 2} (3x^2 - 4x + 1)\).
2. **Apply Limit Laws**: Find \(\lim_{x \to 1} \frac{x^2 - 1}{x - 1}\).
3. **Use the Squeeze Theorem**: Prove that \(\lim_{x \to 0} x^2 \sin\left(\frac{1}{x}\right) = 0\).

### 2. Chemistry: Stoichiometry

#### Key Concepts and Definitions
- **Stoichiometry**: The study of the quantitative relationships between reactants and products in chemical reactions.
- **Mole**: The amount of substance that contains as many elementary entities (atoms, molecules, ions, etc.) as there are atoms in 12 grams of carbon-12.
- **Molar Mass**: The mass of one mole of a substance.
- **Balanced Equation**: An equation that shows the correct proportions of reactants and products in a chemical reaction.

#### Important Formulas or Principles
- **Mole Ratio**: The ratio of the coefficients of reactants and products in a balanced chemical equation.
- **Molar Mass**: Calculated as the sum of the atomic masses of all atoms in a molecule.
- **Stoichiometric Calculations**: Using the mole ratio to determine the amount of a substance required or produced in a reaction.

#### Common Mistakes to Avoid
- **Incorrect Mole Ratios**: Using the wrong coefficients from the balanced equation.
- **Misuse of Molar Mass**: Using the wrong atomic masses or not converting units correctly.
- **Ignoring Units**: Failing to convert between grams, moles, and other units.

#### Practice Problem Types
1. **Calculate Molar Mass**: Find the molar mass of \( \text{Na}_2\text{SO}_4 \).
2. **Use Mole Ratios**: Given the balanced equation \( \text{Fe} + \text{O}_2 \rightarrow \text{Fe}_2\text{O}_3 \), how many moles of oxygen are needed to react with 2 moles of iron?
3. **Stoichiometric Calculations**: If 5 grams of hydrogen react with excess oxygen, how many grams of water are produced?

### 3. Physics: Newton's Laws of Motion

#### Key Concepts and Definitions
- **Newton's First Law (Inertia)**: An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion with the same speed and in the same direction, unless acted upon by an unbalanced force.
- **Newton's Second Law (F = ma)**: The acceleration of an object as produced by a net force is directly proportional to the magnitude of the net force, in the same direction as the net force, and inversely proportional to the mass of the object.
- **Newton's Third Law (Action and Reaction)**: For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.

#### Important Formulas or Principles
- **F = ma**: Force equals mass times acceleration.
- **Action and Reaction**: Forces always occur in pairs, and the forces are equal in magnitude and opposite in direction.

#### Common Mistakes to Avoid
- **Misapplying F = ma**: Forgetting to consider the direction of the force or acceleration.
- **Ignoring the Third Law**: Failing to recognize that action and reaction forces act on different objects.
- **Incorrect Force Analysis**: Not considering all forces acting on an object.

#### Practice Problem Types
1. **Calculate Force**: A 10 kg object is accelerated at 2 m/s². What is the net force acting on the object?
2. **Apply F = ma**: A 5 kg mass is acted upon by a force of 15 N. What is the acceleration of the mass?
3. **Action and Reaction**: A person pushes a 100 kg cart with a force of 50 N. What is the force exerted by the cart on the person?
